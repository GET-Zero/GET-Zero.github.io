GET-Zero

Graph Embodiment Transformer for
Zero-shot Embodiment Generalization

GET-Zero enables zero-shot control of previously unseen robot designs.

Abstract

This paper introduces GET-Zero, a model architecture and training procedure for learning an embodiment-aware control policy that can immediately adapt to new hardware changes without retraining. To do so, we present Graph Embodiment Transformer (GET), a transformer model that leverages the embodiment graph connectivity as a learned structural bias in the attention mechanism. We use behavior cloning to distill demonstration data from embodiment-specific expert policies into an embodiment-aware GET model that conditions on the hardware configuration of the robot to make control decisions. We conduct a case study on a dexterous in-hand object rotation task using different configurations of a four-fingered robot hand with joints removed and with link length extensions. Using the GET model along with a self-modeling loss enables GET-Zero to zero-shot generalize to unseen variation in graph structure and link length, yielding a 20% improvement over baseline methods.

Results

For the original hand design (seen during training), GET-Zero achieves higher average rotational velocity relative to the baseline. GET-Zero includes a graph encoding of the embodiment structure and a self-modeling meta-task that predicts forward kinematics of each joint, both of which yield better performance. The baseline does not have either of these features.

ET (Baseline) - 12 degrees/second

For an embodiment not seen during training with a missing middle finger and a shortened thumb, GET-Zero again outperforms the baseline. We observe that the baseline struggles to keep the cube in a stable pose, in part due to having no knowledge of the embodiment graph structure.

GET-Zero (Ours) - 22 degrees/second

ET (Baseline) - 9 degrees/second

During training time, we train on a variety of hands with joints and links removed. Despite never training on any link length variations, we observe that GET-Zero is able to still control a hand with 1.5cm extensions (shown in orange). In contrast, the baseline does not rotate the cube as fast and drops the cube more frequently.

Failure Cases

The sim-to-real zero-shot control of a previously unseen embodiment is challenging for contact-rich tasks, especially with both graph and link length variations present. We observe a few failure cases with GET-Zero.

The hand below has a missing index finger that creates a gap where the cube can fall through. This compounds any control errors which can come from the fact that link length extensions were never seen during training.


Next, a hand with many link length extensions and a graph structure not seen during training has difficulty stabilizing the cube during the rotation cycle. This is partially due to a pinky finger that only has a single joint, making it difficult to start rotating the cube. Additionally, GET-Zero uses a proprioception-only input (no explicit cube state) to minimize the sim-to-real gap, but this makes it challenging for the policy to adapt to repeated failures.
